Part 1

What is personality?

Personality refers to individual differences in thinking, feeling, and behaving that remain relatively stable over an extended period of time and are consistent across different environments and situations (Haslam & Smilie, 2022).

Why should we care about personality?

Discussion of personality can dominate our daily conversations and has done so since 319 BC in which the tremendous Theophrastus pondered “Often before now have I applied my thoughts to the puzzling questions – why it is that , while all Greece lies under the same sky and all the Greeks are educated alike, It has befallen us to have characters so variously constituted”. Whether it be over tea and biscuits with friends or catching up on the latest gossip with work colleagues in the lunchroom, we have probably all attempted to describe our own or another person's fundamental tendencies. We are social animals that are motivated to understand how we are like all people, like some people, or like no other person, Murray, and Kluckhohn (1953).

Personality Traits

Personality traits are often used as the descriptive unit of personality. In psychology a personality trait is quite specific and must follow a few rules (Haslam & Smilie, 2022).

Principle Description
Psychological not physical Refers to psychological phenomena, e.g. kind not Tall.
Enduring not transient Refers to an enduring trait consistent across time and situation, not a fleeting emotion. E.g. warm not sad.
Broad not specific Broadly describes behaviours across a number of situations. E.g. disciplined not pizza lover.

Describing personality, a common mishap.

Frankfurt’s tennis peers describe him as sociable because his mouth does triple the amount of flapping at the tennis club. Frankfurt also frequents church for gospel studies and the countryside to attend bird watching seminar. However Frankfurt is much quieter and reserved at church and birdwatching. Because this talkativeness appears only around the tennis club, it reflects situational exclusivity and does not hold up to the across-environments test.

Remarkable Raymond Cattell

Cattell also believed aspects of human personality are important have been rcorded in langugae, but went on to hypothesise that traits can precide real world outcomes like job success (Cattell, R.B, 1943). Through using vaguely the following steps the 16PF was born. Step 1: Sort 17 953 trait terms into clusters of synonyms and antonyms Step 2: Matched synonyms with antonyms Step 3: Got 100 people to rate 2 other people they know on a scale regarding a trait. Step 4: examined the correlations between words e.g. people who rated someone else as warm also had high positive correlation with another trait term like kind. Conversely, the antonyms had a high negative correlation with its antonym if someone was rated as warm, it was high likely this had a high negative correlation with an antoymn like cold. Step 5: 16PF that describe core individual differences, that comes with known predictive, construct and test-retest reliability metrics.
