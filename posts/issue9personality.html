What is personality?

Personality refers to individual differences in thinking, feeling, and behaving that remain relatively stable over an extended period of time and are consistent across different environments and situations, (Haslam & Smilie, 2022).

Why should we be fussed about personality?

Discussion around personality dominate our daily conversations and done so since 319 BC in which the tremendous Theophrastus pondered “Often before now have I applied my thoughts to the puzzling questions – why it is that , while all Greece lies under the same sky and all the Greeks are educated alike, It has befallen us to have characters so variously constituted”. Whether it be over tea and biscuits with friends or catching up on the latest gossip with work colleagues in the lunchroom, we have probably all attempted to describe our own or another person's fundamental tendencies. We are social animals that are motivated to understand how we are like all people, like some people, or like no other person, Murray, and Kluckhohn (1953).

Personality Traits

Personality traits are often used as the descriptive unit of personality. In psychology a personality trait is quite specific and must meet a few rules.

  • Psychological not physical: Refers to psychological phenomena, e.g. kind not tall.
  • Enduring not transient: Refers to an enduring trait consistent across time and situation, and not a fleeting emotion. e.g. warm not sad.
  • Broad not specific: Broadly describes behaviours across a number of situations. E.g. Disciplined not pizza lover.

Describing personality, a common mishap.

Frankfurt’s tennis peers describe him as sociable because his mouth does triple the amount of flapping at the tennis club. Frankfurt also frequents church for gospel studies and the countryside to attend bird watching tours but is much quieter and reserved at church and birdwatching. Because this talkativeness appears only around the tennis club, it reflects situational exclusivity and does not hold up to the across-environments test. Therefore, if Frankfurt’s tennis friends knew him only at tennis, describing Frankfurt as extroverted would not be an average representation regarding how he behaves across environments and situations. Frankfurts behaviour at tennis highlights the power of the situation, which greatly influences our behaviour. Through environmental conditioning we learn to automatically react to cues from the environment, helping us to become efficient agents of our environments and save ourselves some cognitive energy to use on something else as we are all managing a limit energy budget. Example, when you see a green traffic light, you most likely don’t put too much though into putting your pedal to the metal, rather it is an automatic reflex that is associated with the positive outcome of getting to work on time. Because of this, our behaviour is often highly environmental or situation specific.
